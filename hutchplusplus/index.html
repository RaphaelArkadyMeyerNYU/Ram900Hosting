Hutch++ for Undergrads

I believe that the analysis of both Hutchinson's Estimator and Hutch++ is simple and approachable. This section contains the analysis an advanced undergraduate should hopefully be able to understand.

Part 1: The Fundamental Shape of Hutchinson's Estimator

Definition 1: Hutchinson's Estimator
Definition

Fix A∈Rn×n\boldsymbol{ A} \in \mathbb R^{n \times n}A∈Rn×n be a real matrix, and fix ℓ∈N\ell\in\mathbb Nℓ∈N. Let x1,…,xℓ∈Rn\mathbf{ x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{ x}_\ell \in\mathbb R^nx1​,…,xℓ​∈Rn be vectors drawn with i.i.d. N(0,1)\mathcal N(0,1)N(0,1) entries. Then, Hutchinson's Estimator is

Hℓ(A) := 1ℓ∑i=1ℓxi⊺Axi H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A}) \;{\vcentcolon=}\; \frac1\ell \sum_{i=1}^\ell \mathbf{ x}_i^\intercal \boldsymbol{ A} \mathbf{ x}_i Hℓ​(A):=ℓ1​i=1∑ℓ​xi⊺​Axi​

We can easily verify two properties of this classical estimator for symmetric A\boldsymbol{ A}A:

Lemma 1: Hutchinson's Mean and Variance
Lemma

E[Hℓ(A)]=tr(A)\mathbb E[H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})] = \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})E[Hℓ​(A)]=tr(A) and Var[Hℓ(A)]=2ℓ∥A∥F2\text{Var}[H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})]=\frac2\ell\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_{ F}^2Var[Hℓ​(A)]=ℓ2​∥A∥F2​

Proof. The expectation is easy to verify:

E[Hℓ(A)]=E[x⊺Ax]=∑i=1n∑j=1nE[[A]i,jxixj]=∑i=1nAi,i=tr(A) \mathbb E[H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})] = \mathbb E[\mathbf{ x}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\mathbf{ x}] = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb E[[\boldsymbol{ A}]_{i,j}\mathbf{ x}_i\mathbf{ x}_j] = \sum_{i=1}^n \boldsymbol{ A}_{i,i} = \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) E[Hℓ​(A)]=E[x⊺Ax]=i=1∑n​j=1∑n​E[[A]i,j​xi​xj​]=i=1∑n​Ai,i​=tr(A)

In order to analyze the variance, we present the analysis of Lemma 9 from Avron, Toledo (2011). Let A=UΛU⊺\boldsymbol{ A}=\boldsymbol{ U}\boldsymbol{ \Lambda}\boldsymbol{ U}^\intercalA=UΛU⊺ be the eigendecomposition of A\boldsymbol{ A}A, and let y := U⊺x\mathbf{ y} \;{\vcentcolon=}\; \boldsymbol{ U}^\intercal\mathbf{ x}y:=U⊺x. Then, for x∼N(0,I)\mathbf{ x}\sim\mathcal N(0,\boldsymbol{ I})x∼N(0,I), we have y∼N(0,UU⊺)=N(0,I)\mathbf{ y} \sim\mathcal N(0,\boldsymbol{ U}\boldsymbol{ U}^\intercal) = \mathcal N(0,\boldsymbol{ I})y∼N(0,UU⊺)=N(0,I). Therefore,

Var[Hℓ(A)]=1ℓVar[x⊺Ax]=1ℓVar[x⊺UΛU⊺x]=1ℓVar[(U⊺x)⊺Λ(Ux)]=1ℓVar[y⊺Λy]=1ℓVar[∑i=1nλiyi2]=1ℓ∑i=1nVar[λiyi2]=1ℓ∑i=1n2λi2=2ℓ∥A∥F2\begin{aligned} \text{Var}[H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})] &= \frac1\ell \text{Var}[\mathbf{ x}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\mathbf{ x}] \\ &= \frac1\ell \text{Var}[\mathbf{ x}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ U}\boldsymbol{ \Lambda}\boldsymbol{ U}^\intercal\mathbf{ x}] \\ &= \frac1\ell \text{Var}[(\boldsymbol{ U}^\intercal\mathbf{ x})^\intercal\boldsymbol{ \Lambda}(\boldsymbol{ U}\mathbf{ x})] \\ &= \frac1\ell \text{Var}[\mathbf{ y}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ \Lambda}\mathbf{ y}] \\ &= \frac1\ell \text{Var}[\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i y_i^2] \\ &= \frac1\ell \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}[\lambda_i y_i^2] \\ &= \frac1\ell \sum_{i=1}^n 2\lambda_i^2 \\ &= \frac2\ell \| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_{ F}^2 \end{aligned}Var[Hℓ​(A)]​=ℓ1​Var[x⊺Ax]=ℓ1​Var[x⊺UΛU⊺x]=ℓ1​Var[(U⊺x)⊺Λ(Ux)]=ℓ1​Var[y⊺Λy]=ℓ1​Var[i=1∑n​λi​yi2​]=ℓ1​i=1∑n​Var[λi​yi2​]=ℓ1​i=1∑n​2λi2​=ℓ2​∥A∥F2​​

Note we cannot just use linearity of trace naively on the sum x⊺Ax=∑i=1n∑j=1nxixjAi,j\mathbf{ x}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\mathbf{ x}=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n x_ix_j \boldsymbol{ A}_{i,j}x⊺Ax=∑i=1n​∑j=1n​xi​xj​Ai,j​ since linearity of variance only holds for independent samples.

■ \blacksquare \, \, ■

This variance is in term of the Frobenius norm of A\boldsymbol{ A}A. So, we expect that Hutchinson's Estimator satisfies Hℓ(A)∈tr(A)±2ℓ∥A∥FH_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A}) \in \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) \pm \frac{\sqrt2}{\sqrt\ell} \| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_{ F}Hℓ​(A)∈tr(A)±ℓ​2​​∥A∥F​ with high probability.[1] Typically in computer science we are given an error tolerance ε\varepsilonε, and want to know how many matrix-vector products we need to compute. (i.e. how small can we make ℓ\ellℓ while still being accurate?) In order to build a guarantee here, we assume A\boldsymbol{ A}A is positive semi-definite (PSD), which in turn implies that ∥A∥F≤tr(A)\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_{ F} \leq \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})∥A∥F​≤tr(A). With this in mind, we can prove the standard result for Hutchinson's Estimator:

Lemma 2: Hutchinson's Estimator
Lemma

Fix A∈Rd×d\boldsymbol{ A} \in \mathbb R^{d \times d}A∈Rd×d be a PSD matrix. Then, Var[Hℓ(A)]≤2ℓtr2(A)\text{Var}[H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})]\leq\frac{2}{\ell}\text{tr}^2(\boldsymbol{ A})Var[Hℓ​(A)]≤ℓ2​tr2(A)

So, if we want to ensure Hℓ(A)∈(1±ε)tr(A)H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})\in(1\pm\varepsilon)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})Hℓ​(A)∈(1±ε)tr(A) with good probability, we need the standard deviation to have 2ℓtr(A)≤εtr(A)\frac{\sqrt 2}{\sqrt \ell}\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) \leq \varepsilon \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})ℓ​2​​tr(A)≤εtr(A), so we need ℓ=O(1ε2)\ell=O(\frac{1}{\varepsilon^2})ℓ=O(ε21​) samples.

Part 2: Hutchinson's versus the Top Few Eigenvalues

This was a section in the first draft of the paper, but was unfortunately cut. This is a slow, methodical intuition for the geometry Hutch++ takes advantage of.

Hutchinson's Estimator is powerfull, and gives a nice rate of convergence. However, the proof of Lemma 2 has a suspicious step. If we write out three lines of analysis:

∣tr(A)−Hℓ(A)∣≤2ℓ∥A∥F(Standard Deviation)≤2ℓtr(A)(∥A∥F≤tr(A))=ε tr(A)(ℓ=O(1ε))\begin{aligned} |\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) - H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})| &\leq {\textstyle\frac{ \sqrt 2}{ \sqrt \ell}} \| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F & \hspace{1cm} & (\text{Standard Deviation})\\ &\leq {\textstyle\frac{ \sqrt 2}{ \sqrt \ell}} \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) & & (\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F \leq \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})) \\ &= \varepsilon \ \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) & & (\ell = O({\textstyle\frac{ 1}{ \varepsilon}})) \\ \end{aligned}∣tr(A)−Hℓ​(A)∣​≤ℓ​2​​∥A∥F​≤ℓ​2​​tr(A)=ε tr(A)​​(Standard Deviation)(∥A∥F​≤tr(A))(ℓ=O(ε1​))​

Looking at these inequalities,

  • The analysis of line 1 is always tight, since we know the variance of Hℓ(A)H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A})Hℓ​(A) exactly.

  • The analysis of line 3 is always tight, since we just set ℓ\ellℓ to the smallest value that gets error ε\varepsilonε.

  • The analysis of line 2 is not always tight.

The second line is tight only if ∥A∥F≈tr(A)\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F \approx \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})∥A∥F​≈tr(A). That is, the analysis above only tells us that Hutchinson's Estimator needs O(1ε)O(\frac1\varepsilon)O(ε1​) samples if A\boldsymbol{ A}A is the kind of matrix that has ∥A∥F≈tr(A)\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F \approx \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})∥A∥F​≈tr(A). So, what kind of matrix is that?

Claim 1: Eigenvalue Intuition
Claim

If ∥A∥F≈tr(A)\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F\approx\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})∥A∥F​≈tr(A), then A\boldsymbol{ A}A has very few large eigenvalues, followed by much smaller eigenvalues.

Try to show this yourself. If you're having trouble, look at the argument below.

Proof. First, we rewrite ∥A∥F\| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F∥A∥F​ and tr(A)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})tr(A) explicitly in terms of the eigenvalues of A\boldsymbol{ A}A. Let v=[λ1,λ2,…,λd]\mathbf{ v} = [\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_d]v=[λ1​,λ2​,…,λd​] be the eigenvalues of A\boldsymbol{ A}A. Then, recall that

∥A∥F=∥v∥2andtr(A)=∑i=1dλi=∥v∥1 \| \boldsymbol{ A}\|_F = \| \mathbf{ v}\|_2 \hspace{0.75cm} and \hspace{0.75cm} \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) = {\textstyle \sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_i} = \| \mathbf{ v}\|_1 ∥A∥F​=∥v∥2​andtr(A)=∑i=1d​λi​=∥v∥1​

where the last equality follows from A\boldsymbol{ A}A being PSD. So, we are given that ∥v∥2≈∥v∥1\| \mathbf{ v}\|_2 \approx \| \mathbf{ v}\|_1∥v∥2​≈∥v∥1​: this is now just a statement about vectors in Rd\mathbb R^dRd!

We now need to show that if a vector v\mathbf{ v}v has ∥v∥2≈∥v∥1\| \mathbf{ v}\|_2 \approx \| \mathbf{ v}\|_1∥v∥2​≈∥v∥1​, then v\mathbf{ v}v is nearly sparse: it has only a few large values.

There are many intuition, and we will look at the extreme, and assume that ∥v∥2=∥v∥1\| \mathbf{ v}\|_2=\| \mathbf{ v}\|_1∥v∥2​=∥v∥1​ exactly. Prove that v\mathbf{ v}v has at most 1 nonzero element in it.

■ \blacksquare \, \, ■

We now finish the discussion with a comparison of two ideas:

  • So, Hutchinson's Estimator only needs to use many samples (i.e. O(1ε)O(\frac1\varepsilon)O(ε1​) samples) if A\boldsymbol{ A}A has very special structure: it has a small number of large eigenvalues.

  • If a matrix has a small number of large eigenvalues, the trace must be well approximated by the sum of those eigenvalues.

With this, we conclude the fundamental intuition behind Hutch++:

Claim 2: Hutch++ Intuition
Claim

If A\boldsymbol{ A}A is the kind of matrix that is hard for Hutchinsons's Estimator to handle, then tr(A)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})tr(A) is well approximated by the top few eigenvalues of A\boldsymbol{ A}A

This leads us to pick the following rough design of an algorithm:

  1. Find a good low-rank approximation A~k\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_kA~k​

  2. Notice that tr(A)=tr(A~k)+tr(A−A~k)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) = \text{tr}(\tilde \boldsymbol{ A}_k) + \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A} - \tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k)tr(A)=tr(A~k​)+tr(A−A~k​)

  3. Compute tr(A~k)\text{tr}(\tilde \boldsymbol{ A}_k)tr(A~k​) exactly

  4. Approximate tr(A−A~k)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}-\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k)tr(A−A~k​) with Hutchinson's Estimator

  5. Return Hutch++(A)=tr(A~k)+Hℓ(A−A~k)\text{Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A}) = \text{tr}(\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k) + H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A}-\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k)Hutch++(A)=tr(A~k​)+Hℓ​(A−A~k​)

In the next section, we state a formal version of Claim 1 (see Lemma 3), show how to compute such a matrix A~k\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_kA~k​ (see QQ⊺A\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}QQ⊺A in Theorem 1), and bound the complexity of the Hutch++ estimator (see Theorem 2).

Part 3: The Variance of Hutch++

Special thanks to Joel A. Tropp for working most of this out, and encouraging us to look into sharpening the constants in the analysis of Hutch++.

By expressing the variance of Hutch++ with all of its constants laid bare, and by using a very simple analysis, this analysis will hopefully allow practitioners to easily the exact parameters in Hutch++ code.

Before bounding the variance of Hutch++, we include the proof of one lemma and import another theorem:

Now, we proceed to the analysis of the variance of Hutch++:

Theorem 2: Variance of Hutch++
Theorem

Fix parameters kkk and ℓ\ellℓ. Let p=2k+1p=2k+1p=2k+1 and construct Q∈Rn×k+p\boldsymbol{ Q}\in\mathbb R^{n \times k+p}Q∈Rn×k+p as in Theorem 1. Then let

Then,

E[Hutch++(A)]=tr(A) \mathbb E[\text{Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A})] = \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A}) E[Hutch++(A)]=tr(A) Var[Hutch++(A)]≤1kℓtr2(A) \text{Var}[\text{Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A})] \leq \frac{1}{k\ell}\text{tr}^2(\boldsymbol{ A}) Var[Hutch++(A)]≤kℓ1​tr2(A)

Proof.

First, recall the Conditional Variance Formula, which says for any random variables X,YX,YX,Y,

Var[Y]=E[Var[Y∣X]]+Var[E[Y∣X]] \text{Var}[Y] = \mathbb E[\text{Var}[Y|X]] + \text{Var}[\mathbb E[Y|X]] Var[Y]=E[Var[Y∣X]]+Var[E[Y∣X]]

Taking Y=Hutch++(A)Y=\text{Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A})Y=Hutch++(A) and X=QX=\boldsymbol{ Q}X=Q, we can bound the variance of Hutch++:

Var[Hutch++]=E[Var[Hutch++∣Q]]+Var[E[Hutch++∣Q]] \text{Var}[\text{Hutch++}] = \mathbb E[\text{Var}[\text{Hutch++} | \boldsymbol{ Q}]] + \text{Var}[\mathbb E[\text{Hutch++} | \boldsymbol{ Q}]] Var[Hutch++]=E[Var[Hutch++∣Q]]+Var[E[Hutch++∣Q]]

The second term above is always zero: Var[E[Hutch++(A)∣Q]]=Var[E[tr(Q⊺AQ)+Hℓ((I−QQ⊺)A)∣Q]]=Var[tr(Q⊺AQ)+tr((I−QQ⊺)A)]=Var[tr(A)]=0\begin{aligned} \text{Var}[\mathbb E[\text{Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A}) | \boldsymbol{ Q}]] &= \text{Var}\left[\mathbb E[\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q}) + H_\ell( (\boldsymbol{ I}-\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal)\boldsymbol{ A}) | \boldsymbol{ Q}]\right] \\ &= \text{Var}\left[\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q}) + \text{tr}((\boldsymbol{ I}-\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal)\boldsymbol{ A}) \right] \\ &= \text{Var}\left[\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})\right] \\ &= 0 \end{aligned}Var[E[Hutch++(A)∣Q]]​=Var[E[tr(Q⊺AQ)+Hℓ​((I−QQ⊺)A)∣Q]]=Var[tr(Q⊺AQ)+tr((I−QQ⊺)A)]=Var[tr(A)]=0​ So, we just need to bound the first term from the conditional variance formula: E[Var[Hutch++∣Q]]=E[Var[tr(Q⊺AQ)+Hℓ((I−QQ⊺)A)∣Q]]=E[Var[Hℓ((I−QQ⊺)A)∣Q]]≤2ℓE[∥(I−QQ⊺)A∥F2]≤2ℓ(1+kp−1)∥A−Ak∥F2\begin{aligned} \mathbb E[\text{Var}[\text{Hutch++} | \boldsymbol{ Q}]] &= \mathbb E[\text{Var}[\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q})+H_\ell( (\boldsymbol{ I}-\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal)\boldsymbol{ A})|Q]] \\ &= \mathbb E[\text{Var}[H_\ell( (\boldsymbol{ I}-\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal)\boldsymbol{ A})|Q]] \\ &\leq {\textstyle\frac{ 2}{ \ell}} \mathbb E[\| (\boldsymbol{ I}-\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal)\boldsymbol{ A}\|_{ F}^2] \\ &\leq {\textstyle\frac{ 2}{ \ell}} (1+\frac{k}{p-1})\| \boldsymbol{ A}-\boldsymbol{ A}_k\|_{ F}^2 \\ \end{aligned}E[Var[Hutch++∣Q]]​=E[Var[tr(Q⊺AQ)+Hℓ​((I−QQ⊺)A)∣Q]]=E[Var[Hℓ​((I−QQ⊺)A)∣Q]]≤ℓ2​E[∥(I−QQ⊺)A∥F2​]≤ℓ2​(1+p−1k​)∥A−Ak​∥F2​​ Where the first inequality uses the variance of Hutchinson's Estimator, and the second uses Theorem 1. Recall that we set p=k+1p=k+1p=k+1. After substituting and simplifying the expression, we find

E[Var[Hutch++∣Q]]≤4ℓ∥A−Ak∥F2 \mathbb E[\text{Var}[\text{Hutch++} | \boldsymbol{ Q}]] \leq {\textstyle\frac{ 4}{ \ell}}\| \boldsymbol{ A}-\boldsymbol{ A}_k\|_{ F}^2 E[Var[Hutch++∣Q]]≤ℓ4​∥A−Ak​∥F2​

And by Lemma 3, we complete the proof:

4ℓ∥A−Ak∥F2≤4ℓ⋅14ℓtr2(A)=1kℓtr2(A) {\textstyle\frac{ 4}{ \ell}}\| \boldsymbol{ A}-\boldsymbol{ A}_k\|_{ F}^2 \leq {\textstyle\frac{ 4}{ \ell}} \cdot {\textstyle\frac{ 1}{ 4\ell}}\text{tr}^2(\boldsymbol{ A}) = {\textstyle\frac{ 1}{ k\ell}}\text{tr}^2(\boldsymbol{ A}) ℓ4​∥A−Ak​∥F2​≤ℓ4​⋅4ℓ1​tr2(A)=kℓ1​tr2(A)

Part 4: Practitioner Advice

The variance in Theorem 2 still has two parameters in it, which leave a bit of ambiguity for practitioners. So, we quickly analyze the choice of kkk and ℓ\ellℓ that minimizes our bound on the variance.

Formally, suppose we are allowed to compute exactly mmm matrix-vector products with A\boldsymbol{ A}A. Then,

  • Computing Q\boldsymbol{ Q}Q uses k+p=2k+1k+p=2k+1k+p=2k+1 products

  • Computing tr(Q⊺AQ)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q})tr(Q⊺AQ) uses k+p=2k+1k+p=2k+1k+p=2k+1 products

  • Computing Hℓ((I−QQ⊺)A)H_\ell( (\boldsymbol{ I}-\boldsymbol{ Q}\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal)\boldsymbol{ A})Hℓ​((I−QQ⊺)A) uses ℓ\ellℓ products

So, we have m=2(2k+1)+ℓ=4k+2+ℓm=2(2k+1)+\ell=4k+2+\ellm=2(2k+1)+ℓ=4k+2+ℓ. We can then verify that k=m−28k=\frac{m-2}{8}k=8m−2​ and ℓ=m2−1\ell=\frac{m}{2}-1ℓ=2m​−1 minimizes the variance. Notably, this is equivalent to setting ℓ=4k\ell=4kℓ=4k, which looks more intuitive. This produces a final variance of Var[Hutch++(A)]≤16(m−2)2tr2(A)\text{Var}[\text{Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A})]\leq\frac{16}{(m-2)^2}\text{tr}^2(\boldsymbol{ A})Var[Hutch++(A)]≤(m−2)216​tr2(A).

Nyström-Hutch++

One additional approach to implementing Hutch++ for PSD matrices considers the Nyström method, where we use the approximation

A~k=(AQ)(Q⊺AQ)−1(AQ)⊺ \tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k = (\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q})(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q})^{-1}(\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q})^{\intercal} A~k​=(AQ)(Q⊺AQ)−1(AQ)⊺

Note that we can compute A~k=Y(Q⊺Y)−1Y⊺\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k=\boldsymbol{ Y}(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercalA~k​=Y(Q⊺Y)−1Y⊺ where Y=AQ\boldsymbol{ Y}=\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q}Y=AQ contains all the matrix-vector products with A\boldsymbol{ A}A. We can compute tr(A~k)\text{tr}(\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k)tr(A~k​) efficiently:

tr(A~k)=tr(Y(Q⊺Y)−1Y⊺)=tr((Q⊺Y)−1(Y⊺Y))\begin{aligned} \text{tr}(\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k) &= \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ Y}(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercal) \\ &= \text{tr}((\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}(\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})) \end{aligned}tr(A~k​)​=tr(Y(Q⊺Y)−1Y⊺)=tr((Q⊺Y)−1(Y⊺Y))​

The matrix inversion is only for a (k+p)×(k+p)(k+p) \times (k+p)(k+p)×(k+p) sized matrix, which is easy. We can then define the NYS-Hutch++ estimator as

NYS-Hutch++(A) := tr((Q⊺Y)−1(Y⊺Y))+Hℓ(A−A~k)\begin{aligned} \text{NYS-Hutch++}(\boldsymbol{ A}) &\;{\vcentcolon=}\; \text{tr}((\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}(\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})) + H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A}-\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k) \\ \end{aligned}NYS-Hutch++(A)​:=tr((Q⊺Y)−1(Y⊺Y))+Hℓ​(A−A~k​)​

We can write out this Hutchinson's Estimator step as

Hℓ(A−A~k)=1ℓ(tr(G⊺AG)−tr(R⊺(Q⊺Y)−1R)) H_\ell(\boldsymbol{ A}-\tilde\boldsymbol{ A}_k) = \frac{1}{\ell}\left(\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ G}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ G}) - \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ R}^\intercal(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}\boldsymbol{ R}) \right) Hℓ​(A−A~k​)=ℓ1​(tr(G⊺AG)−tr(R⊺(Q⊺Y)−1R))

where G\boldsymbol{ G}G has random sign-bit entries and R := Y⊺G\boldsymbol{ R}\;{\vcentcolon=}\;\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ G}R:=Y⊺G. Formally, we can write this out as the following algorithm:

Algorithm 1: NYS-Hutch++

---

input: Matrix-Vector Oracle access to A∈Rd×d\boldsymbol{ A}\in\mathbb R^{d \times d}A∈Rd×d. Number mmm of queries.

output: Approximation to tr(A)\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ A})tr(A)

  1. Sample S∈Rd×m4\boldsymbol{ S}\in\mathbb R^{d \times \frac{m}{4}}S∈Rd×4m​ and G∈Rd×m2\boldsymbol{ G}\in\mathbb R^{d \times \frac{m}{2}}G∈Rd×2m​ with i.i.d. {+1,−1}\{+1,-1\}{+1,−1} entries.

  2. Compute an orthonormal basis Q∈Rd×m4\boldsymbol{ Q}\in\mathbb R^{d \times \frac{m}{4}}Q∈Rd×4m​ for the span of AS\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ S}AS

  3. Compute Y=AQ\boldsymbol{ Y}=\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ Q}Y=AQ and R=Y⊺G\boldsymbol{ R}=\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ G}R=Y⊺G

  4. return tr((Q⊺Y)−1(Y⊺Y))+2m(tr(G⊺AG)−tr(R⊺(Q⊺Y)−1R))\text{tr}((\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}(\boldsymbol{ Y}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})) + \frac{2}{m}\left(\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ G}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ A}\boldsymbol{ G}) - \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ R}^\intercal(\boldsymbol{ Q}^\intercal\boldsymbol{ Y})^{-1}\boldsymbol{ R}) \right)tr((Q⊺Y)−1(Y⊺Y))+m2​(tr(G⊺AG)−tr(R⊺(Q⊺Y)−1R))

Or, in matlab:

function trace_est=simple_nystrom_hutchplusplus(A, num_queries)
    S = 2*randi(2,size(A,1),ceil(num_queries/4))-3;
    G = 2*randi(2,size(A,1),floor(num_queries/2))-3;
    [Q,~] = qr(A*S,0);
    Y = A*Q;
    R = Y' * G;
    QYinv = pinv(Q' * Y);
    trace_est = trace(QYinv * (Y'*Y)) + 1/size(G,2)*[trace(G'*A*G) - trace(R'*QYinv*R)];
end
